Philosophy

Human behavior is never context-free.

People don't learn, work, heal, or interact with technology in isolation. Every human experience emerges from the interaction of brain, body, culture, environment, relationships, identity, and lived history. When these factors are ignored — even well-intentioned systems cause harm. We begin where most design ends.

Futures we are thinking about

The questions shaping
what comes next

We don't only respond to current problems. We anticipate the human dimensions of emerging futures.

F — 01 Active Research

Cognitive liberty

As neurotechnology advances, who owns the data of your mind? We explore the emerging ethical landscape of mental privacy, cognitive enhancement, and the right to mental self-determination.

F — 02 Active Research

Human–AI collaboration

AI is not replacing human cognition — it is reshaping it. We study how humans and intelligent systems can be designed to augment each other without eroding autonomy or meaning.

F — 03 Active Research

Future learning systems

The institutions that shape minds are built for a world that no longer exists. We design learning architectures for the cognitive and cultural demands of the 21st century.

F — 04 Active Research

Digital wellbeing

Attention, affect, and identity are being mediated through screens at unprecedented scale. We examine what healthy human-technology relationships can actually look like.

F — 05 Active Research

Neurotechnology & society

Brain-computer interfaces, neural monitoring, and cognitive augmentation are no longer science fiction. We map their human, social, and ethical implications before they arrive at scale.

F — 06 Active Research

Emergence & complexity

Human systems — cities, organizations, cultures, ecosystems — exhibit emergence. We apply complexity science to understand how to design for resilience rather than fragility.
